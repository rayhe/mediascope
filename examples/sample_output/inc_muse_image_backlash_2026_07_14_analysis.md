## Analysis Notes

This article from Inc.com (not one of the 5 tracked publications) was selected
because it exercises multiple framing patterns the toolkit should detect,
particularly around product reversal narratives.

### Patterns added or extended from this article

1. **confession_framing** (post-quote attribution): Pattern for confession verbs
   (admits, conceded, acknowledged) appearing after quoted speech rather than before.
   Example: "'We missed the mark,' Meta admits."

2. **cross_publication_import** (named publication): Pattern for "According to
   [named major publication]" references that import authority from external outlets.
   Example: "According to *The New York Times*, Meta is pricing..."

3. **policy_reversal** (temporal urgency): Pattern for urgency qualifiers
   (just/merely/only N days after) that editorialize the speed of a reversal.
   Example: "shelving it just three days after its debut"

4. **loaded_language** (death/termination metaphors): Patterns for violent/fatal
   metaphors applied to product decisions: "pulled the plug", "killed the feature",
   "axed the project", "death knell for". Neutral alternatives like "discontinued"
   or "removed" do not trigger.

### Key editorial choices in this article

- Headline uses "Hits Pause" (euphemistic understatement vs body's "pulled the plug")
- Subhead uses confession framing: "Meta admits" vs neutral "Meta said"
- "Controversial" applied to the feature (loaded characterization)
- Temporal compression ("just three days") amplifies urgency of reversal
- Cross-publication import from NYT positions Meta's pricing strategy
  in competitive context without the author needing to source it independently
