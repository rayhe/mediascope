RECONSTRUCTED CONTENT (composite from secondary sources):

The Trump administration is pressing Meta to submit its artificial intelligence
models for voluntary government review, which would allow federal authorities to
evaluate the models' capabilities and vulnerabilities, according to four people
familiar with the confidential request.

The request was made in emails between the administration and the social media
giant, as the government steps up oversight of the AI industry amid escalating
national security concerns about the power of frontier AI systems.

Meta is the only major U.S. developer of artificial intelligence technology that
has not reached an agreement to voluntarily share its models with the federal
government for review, according to the report. Other companies — including
OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Microsoft, and Amazon — have already signed similar
voluntary agreements.

The push comes as the White House has increasingly asserted authority over frontier
AI releases. Earlier this month, the government ordered Anthropic to suspend
global access to its most advanced AI models, Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, for
foreign nationals, citing national security concerns about the models' cyber
capabilities. That directive was issued by the Commerce Department on June 12,
just days after Anthropic had released the models to the public.

President Trump signed an executive order on June 2 establishing a voluntary
framework for AI developers to offer "covered frontier models" to the U.S.
government for up to 30 days before releasing them to trusted partners. The
framework is intended to give cybersecurity and national security officials time
to evaluate potential threats before the tools become widely available.

Meta's position is complicated by its open-source approach to AI development.
The company's Llama models are freely available for download, which makes
traditional pre-release government review more challenging than for closed models
like OpenAI's GPT series or Anthropic's Claude. Once released, open-source models
cannot be recalled or restricted in the same way.

In a statement provided to Reuters, Meta said: "We share the administration's
goal of advancing U.S. leadership on robust and secure frontier AI. While we
are working through the details, we hope to sign the agreement soon."

The U.S. Commerce Department did not immediately respond to a request for comment.

The development comes amid a broader shift in Washington's approach to AI
regulation. The government has moved from largely hands-off oversight to actively
intervening in model releases, a change driven by growing concerns about the
cybersecurity capabilities of increasingly powerful AI systems.

Critics have raised concerns that the emerging framework allows the government
to pick winners and losers in the AI industry. Some analysts have pointed to
the administration's past conflicts with Anthropic as evidence that political
considerations may be influencing oversight decisions. OpenAI, whose leadership
has been more aligned with the Trump administration's approach, has received
comparatively favorable treatment in the process.

Brad Carson, head of Public First, a bipartisan pro-AI safety super PAC, told
CNN that the situation demonstrates the need for clear regulations: "Right now,
you have an ad hoc, personalized, opaque, possibly lawless approach. It is
certainly appropriate for the government to recall dangerous products, including
AI models, but it has to be done in a way consistent with transparency and
basic fairness."

The Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), housed within the Commerce
Department, has been the primary government unit reviewing AI models. But the
recent executive order gave cybersecurity and national-security officials a
bigger role in model evaluation, worrying some companies that had been working
more closely with CAISI.

The broader context includes China's growing AI capabilities and alleged
intellectual property theft. Anthropic recently accused Alibaba of conducting
the largest known distillation attack on the company, generating more than
28.8 million exchanges through nearly 25,000 fraudulent accounts between
April and June 2026.
