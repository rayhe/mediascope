--- BEGIN RECONSTRUCTED TEXT ---

Meta's months-old Applied AI unit has triggered deep frustration among staff,
with workers describing the assignments as "soul-crushing" and one employee
comparing the experience to being drafted into "the gulag."

The unit was formed in March 2026 to support research efforts at Meta
Superintelligence Labs. It brought together roughly 6,500 engineers and product
managers — many of whom had previously built features used by billions of people
on Facebook, Instagram, and other Meta platforms.

At a livestreamed internal meeting earlier this month, a disgruntled employee
interrupted a presentation with an expletive-filled tirade about "being the
company's b---h," raving that an unnamed Meta AI executive should be told "he's
a piece of s--t." One of the presenters reportedly covered their face with their
hands before meeting leaders told everyone to hit the mute button.

The incident highlighted growing frustration inside the division. Workers
reported widespread dissatisfaction with how Meta assembled the unit, and several
employees said they felt they had little choice but to join — or leave the
company entirely. Some workers began calling themselves "draftees."

"It's literally the gulag," one unnamed employee told Wired. "You have zero
purpose in life all of a sudden, you barely interact with anyone, you just have
these tasks every week."

"Most people find the work soul-crushing," a second employee said.

Another described the assignments as "mechanical and not creative," complaining
they were "not using their full skill set and knowledge."

The tasks reportedly include creating puzzles, coding challenges, and evaluation
tests designed to assess and improve AI models. Workers described the assignments
as repetitive and disconnected from their earlier product engineering roles.
Some teams had up to 50 workers per manager.

In an internal memo, Meta CTO Andrew Bosworth acknowledged the company had
mishandled aspects of the transition, saying Meta had done "an atrocious job"
communicating its vision and support plans. He said the company had "undermined
trust by failing to clearly explain how careers would evolve under the new
structure."

CEO Mark Zuckerberg also addressed the situation in an internal memo, admitting
that recent changes had "caused distress." He stated that "Meta's north star is
to be the best place for the most talented individuals worldwide to create an
impact" and said there would be no further mass layoffs.

Applied AI Vice President Maher Saba said reassigned employees would be allowed
to apply for other positions within the company.

As part of retention efforts, Meta announced new workplace measures including
improved snack and beverage options, increased travel budgets, more investment in
social events, a cap of 20 direct reports per manager (down from 50), and
optional AI coaching tools.
