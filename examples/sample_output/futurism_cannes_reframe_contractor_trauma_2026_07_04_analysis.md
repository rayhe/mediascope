## 1. Article Summary

Futurism repackages the Wired "Project Cannes" investigation (July 1, 2026)
for its tech-skeptic audience. The article largely re-reports Wired's findings —
hundreds of Meta contractors (via Covalen) posed as teenagers to bombard
competitor AI chatbots with disturbing prompts — but adds two editorial layers
absent from the original:

1. **Contractor trauma narrative** (¶7–9): Landymore connects Cannes to Meta's
   2020 content moderator lawsuit settlement and 2026 Ray-Ban AI glasses privacy
   complaints, constructing a pattern of Meta offloading psychologically harmful
   work onto expendable contractors.

2. **Anticompetitive reframe** (¶10–12): Expert Rumman Chowdhury's closing quote
   reframes the story from "safety testing gone too far" to "anticompetitive
   practices disguised as safety" — a fundamentally different narrative conclusion
   than the Wired original.

1. **Historical pattern stacking** (¶7): The article strings together the 2020

   AND aligned with public interest. This is the opposite of anonymous_authority

### Amplification Pattern

The three articles demonstrate a classic media amplification cascade:
1. **Wired** (Jul 1): Document-heavy investigation. Lets sources speak. Ends with
   Meta's defense. Tone is damning but structured for journalistic credibility.
2. **Newzlet** (Jul 1): Immediately reframes "safety testing" as "competitive
   intelligence." Introduces absence_as_evidence — what Meta *didn't* do
   becomes the argument. Explicitly editorial.
3. **Futurism** (Jul 4): Three days later, adds contractor trauma historical
   pattern stacking and lands on the strongest reframe: not just competitive
   intelligence, but "anticompetitive practices." The Wired investigation's
   careful factual architecture becomes raw material for an explicitly
   adversarial conclusion that the original didn't reach.

Each outlet adds editorial distance from the documents and editorial proximity
to a conclusion the original investigation deliberately avoided. This is how
secondary reporting amplifies investigative exclusives beyond the original
journalist's intent.

### Patterns Added (4 new regex patterns, 397 → 401 total)

1. **loaded_language: contractor exploitation/trauma pattern** — "offloaded,"
   "traumatized," "gobsmacked," "brute force approach," "forced to come up with,"
   "ostensibly in the name of." Captures language framing employer-contractor
   relationships as harmful outsourcing of psychological harm.

2. **loaded_language: expanded secrecy terms** — "secretive," "clandestine"

3. **loaded_language: expanded coercion verbs** — "forced to watch/view/review/
   read/listen/participate" added to existing "forced to install/use/adopt/accept."

4. **cross_publication_import: secondary-reporting attribution** — "per the
   magazine," "per the reporting," "per the investigation," "according to the
   [publication]." Catches how secondary outlets attribute facts to the primary
   investigation.

5. **kicker_framing: anticompetitive/governance patterns** — "anticompetitive,"
   "convenient cover," "governance gray zone," "convenient excuse/pretext/shield/
   fig leaf." Detects articles that end by reframing the subject's actions as
   anticompetitive or corrupt.

6. **geopolitical_regulatory_pressure: defiance context restriction** —
   "defy/defied/defying" separated into its own pattern requiring proximity to
   geopolitical/governmental terms. Eliminates FPs like "defied their guardrails."
