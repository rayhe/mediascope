## Manual Assessment Summary

The NY Post covers the same $1.4T damages disclosure as Reuters and Gizmodo but applies
distinctive tabloid framing techniques. The article is notable for how it smuggles editorial
stance through headline construction, CEO personalization, scale juxtaposition, and — 
uniquely for this event — a tempering coda that anchors expectations in prior verdict
outcomes. It also deploys the "Big Tobacco moment" analogy at paragraph level without
attribution of who coined it.

### Headline Analysis

**"Meta says it's facing $1.4T in penalties in teen mental health case — a sum equal to
tech giant's valuation"**

The headline performs three distinct framing operations:

1. **CEO Personalization (indirect):** While the headline uses "Meta" as subject, the
   body immediately pivots to "The Mark Zuckerberg-led company" (paragraph 2), converting
   the institutional action into CEO-attributed agency. This is indirect CEO personalization
   — the headline primes institutional framing, then the body reframes it through Zuckerberg.

2. **Valuation Comparison (explicit):** The dash-separated subordinate clause "a sum equal
   to tech giant's valuation" deploys `valuation_comparison` framing. By anchoring the
   penalty against market cap *in the headline itself*, the Post converts a legal proceeding
   into an existential corporate narrative before the reader processes a single paragraph.
   This is more aggressive than Gizmodo's "Existential Threat" (which is abstract) because
   it uses a concrete financial equivalence.

3. **Scale/Magnitude:** "$1.4T" uses abbreviated trillion notation — tabloid convention for
   compressing enormous numbers into headline-friendly format. The abbreviation actually
   *reduces* visual shock compared to "1.4 Trillion" (Gizmodo) but makes it scannable.

### Unique Framing: Tempering Coda

Unlike Gizmodo (which ends on escalation: "14 more states have also brought claims... set
to go to trial early next year") or Reuters (which ends on procedural context), the NY Post
deploys a **tempering coda** — a structural technique where the article's final paragraphs
deliberately lower expectations after the shocking headline/lede:

- ¶11: "Still, the $1.4 trillion penalty is likely far higher than what Meta will ultimately face"
- ¶12: KGM case resulted in only $6 million (Meta's share: $4.2M)
- ¶13: New Mexico resulted in $375 million — and Meta spokesman "touted" it as "just a fraction"

This creates a **whiplash structure**: the headline screams existential threat, the body
builds the case with loaded language and scale amplification, then the final three paragraphs
walk it back with concrete anchoring numbers. The rhetorical effect is sophisticated: the
reader absorbs the shock of $1.4 trillion, forms an emotional impression, and only
encounters the "reality check" after the framing has set. Prior verdicts of $6M and $375M
against a $1.4T demand create an implicit ~99.97% discount expectation, but the tempering
comes too late to undo the headline impression.

This technique is not currently tracked by MediaScope. Suggested new device:
**Tempering Coda** — article ends by contextualizing or walking back its own headline-level
framing, creating a structural hedge that technically prevents the piece from being
"misleading" while preserving the dramatic lede effect.

### Tabloid-Specific Techniques

1. **"On the hook for"** (¶1, ¶12): Colloquial debt/obligation language. Less formal than
   "liable for" (legal) or "ordered to pay" (judicial). Creates a sense of inescapable
   personal obligation.

2. **"Face off"** (¶3): Sports/combat metaphor for a courtroom proceeding. Converts
   procedural legal events into adversarial confrontation.

3. **"Blasted"** (¶1): Attribution verb importing physical violence metaphor. "Meta blasted
   as outlandish" is more aggressive than Reuters' neutral "the company said."

4. **"Sex creeps"** (¶11): Tabloid compound noun. More emotionally loaded than NYT/Reuters
   "sexual predators" or legal "persons who sexually exploit minors." Imports disgust
   reaction.

5. **"With Post wires"** (final line): Transparent about wire sourcing, but the framing
   layers — CEO personalization, loaded language, tempering coda — are Post additions to
   the Reuters base.
