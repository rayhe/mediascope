Code discreetly added to Meta's AI app over multiple updates this year shows
that the feature, internally called "NameTag," identifies people captured by
the glasses' camera and, when activated, alerts the wearer when it recognizes
someone.

The Meta AI app, which is used with the company's Ray-Ban and Oakley smart
glasses and has been downloaded more than 50 million times, contains core
components of a face-recognition system that were added as early as January
2026, including three AI models that now sit on users' phones. One model
detects faces, another crops them from images, and a third converts them into
biometric data that can be checked against faceprints stored on the phone.

If activated, the system would alert the wearer when it recognizes someone.
Faces that are recognized trigger notifications, while unrecognized faces are
cropped, indexed, and saved to a folder marked "pending."

A May version of the app appears to rebrand the feature as "Connections,"
with user-facing text inviting people to "remember the people you met."

Cooper Quintin, a security researcher and senior public interest technologist
with the Electronic Frontier Foundation's Threat Lab, who was asked to review
the code by Wired, said the system was "nearly ready to go." Security
researchers cited in the report independently recreated portions of the
system and found that the recognition pipeline appeared nearly functional.

The state of near-readiness seems contradictory compared to Meta's previous
messaging. In April, Meta had publicly described facial recognition as
something the company was still "thinking through" before rolling anything
out. However, the investigation shows some code was already added as early as
January — before Meta made its official comment.

This is not the first time NameTag has surfaced. In February, The New York
Times reported on internal Meta documents showing that the company had planned
a face-recognition feature for its smart glasses. Those documents reportedly
described how the current "dynamic political environment" could leave critics
of the feature preoccupied.

Per a memo reviewed during reporting, Meta was interested in launching the
feature during a "dynamic political environment" in the US because "civil
society groups that we would expect to attack us would have their resources
focused on other concerns."

Meta has already faced legal action over the unlawful collection of biometric
data and the creation of facial recognition profiles without users' explicit
consent. In 2020 and 2024, as part of two class actions in Illinois and
Texas, the company paid $650 million and $1.4 billion respectively to settle
the cases.

Meta pushed back on the framing of the report. Company spokesperson Ryan
Daniels stated: "Regardless of any sensational reporting, the facts are
simple: We've said before we're exploring these types of features, and what
you're seeing is just evidence of that exploration. Nothing has shipped to
consumers and no final decision has been made on what to do here, if anything.
If we do decide to roll something out, we will take a thoughtful approach and
do so with full transparency. One decision we can be clear about — we are not
building a central face database."

Wired notes that code reviewed suggests NameTag would be pulling information
from Meta servers and storing it on a user's device, raising questions about
Daniels' assertion of "not building a central face database."

The underlying technology already present in the app people use with Meta's
smart glasses suggests a potential rollout is moving closer.

[Civil rights response, covered in follow-up reporting:]

A coalition of over 70 civil liberties, domestic violence, reproductive
rights, and LGBTQ+ organizations, including the ACLU, Fight for the Future,
and Access Now, subsequently sent a letter to Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg
demanding the company kill the facial recognition feature before it ever
reaches consumers. The coalition called Meta's internal memo about the
"dynamic political environment" "vile behavior."

Engineers at Meta were reportedly weighing two versions of the feature: one
that identifies people the wearer is already connected with on Meta platforms,
and a broader version that could recognize anyone with a public Facebook or
Instagram account.

On June 8, 2026, Meta released an update to its Meta AI app removing the
facial recognition features linked to its smart glasses, following the
investigation.
