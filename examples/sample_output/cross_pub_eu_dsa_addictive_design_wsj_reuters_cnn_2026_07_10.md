# Same-Event Comparison: EU DSA "Addictive Design" Ruling (July 10, 2026)
## WSJ vs Reuters vs CNN

**Event:** European Commission preliminary finding that Meta's Facebook and Instagram violate the Digital Services Act through "addictive design" features (autoplay, infinite scroll, personalized recommendations).

**Date:** July 10, 2026 (all three articles published same day)

**Articles compared:**
1. **WSJ** — "Meta Failed to Protect Users From Addictive Apps, EU Says" (Kim Mackrael, Brussels)
2. **Reuters** — "EU tells Instagram, Facebook to change addictive features or risk fines"
3. **CNN** — "Facebook and Instagram's 'addictive design' may violate European law, EU Commission finds"

---

## 1. Headline Framing

| Publication | Headline | Framing Choice | Severity Implied |
|-------------|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **WSJ** | "Meta Failed to Protect Users From Addictive Apps, EU Says" | **Failure attribution** — active verb "failed," Meta as subject failing to protect | High — implies moral/systemic failure |
| **Reuters** | "EU tells Instagram, Facebook to change addictive features or risk fines" | **Regulatory command** — EU as subject issuing directives; consequence-focused | Medium — transactional, procedural |
| **CNN** | "Facebook and Instagram's 'addictive design' may violate European law, EU Commission finds" | **Legal finding** — passive/tentative "may violate"; hedging with "finds" | Low-Medium — conditional, preliminary |

**Analysis:** The headline gradient runs from WSJ (most assertive, most negative toward Meta) through Reuters (neutral/procedural) to CNN (most hedged). WSJ's "Failed to Protect" is a loaded construction — it presupposes a duty to protect and declares Meta didn't meet it. Reuters focuses on the regulatory action itself. CNN preserves maximum optionality with "may violate."

This is an important framing pattern: **same set of facts, same day, three different severity levels in headlines.** The facts support all three framings, but the editorial choice about which framing to lead with reveals each outlet's default posture.

| Publication | US Litigation Mentioned | TikTok DSA Precedent | Australia Ban | External Research | Future Ban Possibility |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------------|---------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **WSJ** | ✅ California + New Mexico verdicts | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ |

| **CNN** | ✅ Two US jury verdicts + multiple countries moving on bans | ❌ | ✅ | ✅ NYU/Northeastern safety features report (66% non-functional) | ❌ |

**Analysis:** Each publication contextualizes the EU ruling differently:

- **WSJ** frames it within the US legal landscape (jury verdicts) and US-EU regulatory tensions — reader is a US business audience that cares about cross-jurisdictional risk.
- **Reuters** frames it within the EU regulatory arc (TikTok precedent → Meta → potential Europe-wide teen ban) — reader gets the EU policy trajectory. The exclusive Virkkunen quote and the forward-looking teen ban note make this the most policy-rich of the three.
- **CNN** frames it within the global child safety movement (US verdicts, Australia ban, NYU research) — reader gets the broadest societal context. The NYU/Northeastern research adds an empirical layer: not just "regulators say it's bad" but "independent researchers measured how bad."

**Toolkit implications:** The CNN article's use of the NYU/Northeastern report is a **cross_publication_import** variant — importing academic research to bolster the regulatory finding. The toolkit should track when articles cite independent research vs. only regulatory/corporate sources.

## 4. Fine Quantification

| Publication | Fine Amount Cited | Calculation Method |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **WSJ** | "up to 6% of its global revenue for each platform" | Per-platform |
| **Reuters** | "up to 6% of its global annual turnover" | Single percentage |
| **CNN** | "as much as 6% of its global revenue — which could total more than $12 billion" | Percentage + absolute dollar amount |

**Analysis:** CNN is the only outlet to translate the percentage into an absolute dollar amount ($12B+). This is a **scale_magnitude** framing choice: "$12 billion" hits harder than "6% of revenue" even though they describe the same thing. WSJ's "for each platform" note is the most legally precise. Reuters provides the cleanest regulatory language.

## 7. Toolkit Improvements from Cross-Comparison

### 7.1 Dollar-amount translation as framing device
When a publication converts a percentage-based regulatory fine into an absolute dollar figure, that's a **scale_magnitude** amplification choice. The toolkit detects scale_magnitude for large numbers, but should specifically flag `percentage_to_dollar_conversion` as a sub-pattern.

### 7.2 Academic research import as framing device
CNN's use of the NYU/Northeastern report is a distinct framing choice from citing regulatory findings or corporate statements. Propose sub-type of **cross_publication_import**: `academic_research_import` — when articles cite peer-reviewed or institutional research to validate the article's editorial thesis.

### 7.3 Age-verification doubt insertion
CNN's parenthetical "but it's not clear how well the technology works" about Meta's AI age detection is an **editorial_aside** device — breaking from reported facts to insert doubt. The toolkit should pattern-match parenthetical doubt insertions.

### 7.4 Forward-hook framing
Reuters' exclusive forward look to von der Leyen's September teen ban announcement is a **stakes_escalation** variant: connecting today's finding to a future policy threat. Pattern: "expected to announce" + future date + policy escalation.
