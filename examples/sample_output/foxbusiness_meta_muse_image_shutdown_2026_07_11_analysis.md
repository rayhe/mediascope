## 3. Framing Devices Detected

### Device 1: **Editorial Cross-Promotion** (#67)
**Instances:** 3
- `FOUR STATES SEEKING $1.4 TRILLION IN PENALTIES IN CHILD SOCIAL MEDIA ADDICTION TRIAL, META SAYS`
- `JUDGE LETS STATES PURSUE CLAIMS THAT META DESIGNED FACEBOOK AND INSTAGRAM TO ADDICT CHILDREN`
- `CLICK HERE TO GET FOX BUSINESS ON THE GO`

**Effect:** The two content callouts import the framing of separate legal/regulatory stories into the Muse Image discontinuation story. This creates a **narrative accumulation** effect: the reader encounters the Muse Image shutdown sandwiched between $1.4T in penalties and child addiction claims. The editorial juxtaposition makes a product feature decision feel like part of a pattern of corporate recklessness.

**Analytical significance:** This is the clearest example of editorial_cross_promotion as narrative construction. The callout headlines are:
1. Not relevant to the Muse Image feature (the $1.4T penalty is about addictive design, not AI image generation)
2. Selected from adversarial/negative stories (no positive Meta headlines are cross-promoted)
3. Structurally positioned to bracket the most factual section of the article

### Device 2: **Policy Reversal / Controlled Retreat Language** (#52)
**Instance:** Meta's statement uses all four controlled retreat techniques:
- Intent displacement: "Our intent was to provide a useful creative tool"
- Active listening: "We've heard the feedback"
- Target-miss euphemism: "this feature missed the mark"
- Passive unavailability: "it's no longer available"

**Fox Business treatment:** The article quotes the controlled retreat language in full (3 consecutive sentences). Unlike editorialized outlets, Fox Business does not add commentary unpacking or mocking the retreat language. The meta-critique comes from structural juxtaposition rather than editorial prose.

### Device 3: **Default Burden Privacy** (#66)
**Instance:** "The company said users who did not want their public Instagram content used through the AI feature could disable it through the app's Sharing and Reuse settings."

**Effect:** By including the opt-out mechanics, the article implicitly reinforces the structural critique: the burden is on users to protect themselves.

### Device 4: **Outsourced Intensity** (#3 variant — victim testimony)
**Instance:** Shah's quotes carry all the emotional language:
- "fraud actually happening in real time"
- "older people have been scammed"
- "my image has been used to scam them"
- "I can't do anything about it"

**Effect:** Fox Business maintains neutral editorial prose while outsourcing all emotional intensity to a named, credible source (NIH-funded researcher). This is journalistically defensible but editorially effective: the reporter doesn't have to call Meta reckless when a researcher describes watching elderly people get scammed.

### Device 5: **Refusal Amplification** (#39)
**Instance:** "In response to a request for comment from FOX Business, Meta directed Fox Business to the updated Instagram blog post announcing the change."

**Effect:** Closing the article with Meta's non-engagement emphasizes the company's unwillingness to address the specific harms documented by Shah. Structural kicker position amplifies the effect.

**Key divergence:** Reuters published a 4-sentence wire flash with zero framing devices. ### Fox Business Editorial Technique
Fox Business employs a distinctive style that this analysis documents:
1. **Body text is restrained:** Editorial prose uses neutral verbs ("announced," "introduced," "urged," "said")
2. **Emotional work is outsourced:** Named sources (Shah, SAG-AFTRA) carry all loaded language
3. **Structural framing does the editorial work:** All-caps interstitial callouts to adversarial stories create narrative association WITHOUT editorial prose connecting them
4. **No-comment amplification:** Meta's redirect to blog post is placed at the structural kicker position

This technique is **journalistically defensible** (editorial prose is factual; sources are named and credible) while **editorially effective** (the structural choices embed a negative meta-narrative). It represents a distinct approach from Gizmodo-style direct editorial commentary or Reuters-style pure wire reporting.
